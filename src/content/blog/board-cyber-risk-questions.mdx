The board chair of a community health non-profit raised her hand in the annual risk review meeting. "We talk about financial risk every quarter. We talk about reputational risk. We've never once talked about the risk that someone breaks into our systems and publishes our patient records. Should we be talking about that?"

The room was quiet. The executive director looked at the IT director. The IT director said they had antivirus software. The board moved on.

Six months later, they were notified of a breach. Patient records from 1,200 community health visits had been exfiltrated. The organization had to report to state regulators, notify every affected individual, and explain to funders why it had happened.

The board hadn't asked. But the board also hadn't been given the tools to ask.

## What Good Governance Looks Like

Board members are accountable for organizational risk — including cyber risk. That accountability doesn't require technical expertise. It requires the right questions.

A board member doesn't need to understand what a firewall does to govern cyber risk effectively. They need to understand what questions to ask, what a credible answer sounds like, and when to escalate.

Here are the questions that responsible governance demands.

## The Questions Every Board Should Be Asking

**"What's our most sensitive data, and who has access to it?"**

The answer should be specific. Not "donor data" — how many records, what fields, in what systems, with access by which roles. If leadership can't answer this with specificity, that's a finding.

**"What would happen in the first two hours of a ransomware attack?"**

The answer should include: who gets called, who has decision authority, whether there's a backup to restore from, and whether you have an incident response contact. If the answer involves the phrase "we'd figure it out," that's a finding.

**"Have we tested our backups recently?"**

Most organizations have backups. Fewer organizations have confirmed that those backups actually restore correctly. Untested backups are assumptions, not controls.

**"What security requirements do our major funders have?"**

Federal funders and an increasing number of foundations are building cybersecurity attestation into grant requirements. Not knowing the answer creates compliance risk.

**"Do we know if we've already been breached?"**

Most breaches aren't discovered immediately. The average dwell time — the period between initial compromise and detection — is months. "We haven't had a breach" and "we haven't detected a breach" are very different statements.

**"What's our cyber insurance coverage, and does our current security posture qualify?"**

This is covered in more depth [in our insurance post](/resources/cyber-insurance-policy), but the short version: most organizations haven't read their policy carefully, and many have security gaps that would cause a claim to be denied.

## What Good Answers Look Like

Boards often accept answers that sound reassuring without probing whether they're credible. Here's a rough heuristic:

**Good answer**: Specific, references named systems and processes, includes a number or timeline, acknowledges gaps or open items.

**Concerning answer**: General, uses industry jargon without connecting to your specific environment, implies the question doesn't apply to you ("we're too small to be a target").

**Bad answer**: Defensive, minimizes the question, implies that asking is overreaction.

## What to Do If You're Starting From Zero

If the honest answer to most of these questions is "I don't know," that's not a crisis — it's a baseline. The right response is to establish one: a basic inventory of sensitive data, a documented incident response contact list, a look at your cyber insurance policy.

The goal isn't to become a cybersecurity-proficient board overnight. It's to build enough situational awareness that you can ask the right questions and recognize credible answers — and to make clear to leadership that this is a risk the board takes seriously.
