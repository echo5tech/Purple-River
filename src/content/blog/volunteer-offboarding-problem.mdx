Asha ran the tutoring program at a small literacy non-profit for three years. She was good at her job, deeply trusted, and given access to everything: the donor database, the Google Workspace admin panel, the shared drive with beneficiary records. When she left for a graduate program out of state, there was a going-away party, a card signed by the whole team, and a heartfelt thank-you from the executive director.

No one changed her password. No one revoked her access. No one knew where to start.

Six months later, her Google account — still active, still full-access — sent a phishing email from her organizational address to 400 donors. The email looked legitimate because it was sent from a legitimate address. The damage wasn't catastrophic, but the trust took years to rebuild.

## Why This Keeps Happening

The problem isn't negligence. It's process — or the absence of one.

Non-profits run on the goodwill of volunteers, and that creates a particular culture around access. When someone joins, you give them what they need as quickly as possible. When someone leaves, there's rarely a documented handoff. There's no IT department to file a ticket with. There's no offboarding checklist that includes "revoke Google Workspace access" alongside "return the office key."

The result is identity sprawl: a growing list of accounts tied to people who no longer have a relationship with your organization — or who do, but in a different capacity than when the account was created.

## The Numbers Behind the Risk

The average compromised account sits active for 197 days before it's discovered. For a volunteer account at a non-profit, that window is often much longer — because there's no monitoring, no alert, and no one checking.

Orphaned accounts are one of the most consistently exploited attack vectors across all sectors. For non-profits, they're particularly dangerous because:

- Access was often granted broadly, without role-based restrictions
- There's rarely an access log anyone is reviewing
- The accounts often have legitimate history, making them harder to flag as suspicious
- Attackers who gain access to a real organizational email address can impersonate staff convincingly

## What Closing This Gap Actually Looks Like

You don't need enterprise software to fix this. You need a process.

**An offboarding checklist** that's triggered automatically when someone's term ends — not when someone remembers to trigger it. It should cover every system that person had access to, including the ones that feel minor.

**A regular access audit** — at minimum annually, ideally quarterly — that compares your current volunteer roster against every account in every system you use. Anyone not on the current roster gets a review.

**Principle of least privilege** applied going forward. New volunteers get access to what they need for their role, not access to everything because it's easier to give too much.

**MFA on every account** that accesses sensitive data. Even if an orphaned account exists, a second factor limits what an attacker can do with it.

None of this is technically complex. What makes it hard is making it a habit — and having someone accountable for running the process.
