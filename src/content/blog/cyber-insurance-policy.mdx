The executive director of a food bank in the Midwest got the call at 6:43 on a Tuesday morning. Their server was encrypted. Files were gone. Someone wanted $18,000 in cryptocurrency to send the decryption key.

They had cyber insurance. They'd had it for two years. They called the claims line, answered questions for an hour, and then waited.

The insurer denied the claim.

The reason: their policy required multi-factor authentication on all administrative accounts as a condition of coverage. They had MFA on some accounts. Not all. The gap — three admin logins without it — was enough for the insurer to walk away from a valid claim.

## The Policy You Have vs. The Policy You Think You Have

Cyber insurance has grown quickly as a product category, and the marketing language has grown faster than the policy language has been standardized. The same terms mean different things across different carriers. "Ransomware coverage" can mean very different things depending on what's in the exclusions.

Here's what to look for when you pull up your policy.

## The Definitions Section (Read This First)

Before you look at coverage limits, read the definitions section. Terms like "security event," "data breach," "business interruption," and "ransomware" are defined specifically in your policy — and the definition often determines whether a claim is valid.

Pay particular attention to:

- **"Security event" vs. "data breach"**: These are often defined separately, and coverage may apply to one but not the other.
- **"System"**: Some policies only cover systems you own and operate, not cloud services like Google Workspace or Salesforce Nonprofit.
- **"Third-party liability"**: This covers claims made against you by others whose data was exposed. It's separate from first-party costs (your own recovery costs).

## Conditions of Coverage (The Fine Print That Matters)

This is where many claims fail. Most cyber policies have specific security requirements you must meet to maintain coverage. Common requirements include:

- Multi-factor authentication on email and administrative accounts
- Regular data backups, often with offline or offsite copies
- Current patches on all covered systems
- Annual security training for staff
- Specific password management practices

These aren't suggestions. They're contractual obligations. If you don't meet them when a claim is filed, the insurer can deny coverage — even if the policy is otherwise valid and premiums are current.

Look for the section titled "Conditions," "Warranties," or "Representations" — it's usually near the back of the policy document.

## What's Excluded (The Coverage That Quietly Disappears)

Most policies have a list of exclusions that can be substantial. Common exclusions include:

- **Acts of war or nation-state attacks**: Increasingly contested in courts, but still common language.
- **Prior known conditions**: If you knew about a vulnerability before the policy started, claims related to it may be excluded.
- **Failure to follow your own security policies**: If you have a written security policy (or were asked for one during underwriting) and didn't follow it, that creates exposure.
- **Physical damage**: Data loss that results from hardware failure rather than a cyberattack is often not covered.

## The Coverage You Actually Want

If you're reviewing a policy or renewing, here are the specific coverage types to ask about by name:

- **First-party breach response**: Covers your own forensics, notification costs, and credit monitoring for affected individuals.
- **Business interruption**: Covers lost revenue during downtime. Watch for waiting periods (often 8–24 hours) before this kicks in.
- **Ransomware payment coverage**: Ask explicitly whether ransom payments are covered and whether there are conditions.
- **Regulatory defense and fines**: If you're subject to HIPAA or handle certain types of personal data, ask about regulatory coverage specifically.
- **Social engineering / funds transfer fraud**: Covers wire fraud and business email compromise — increasingly common and often excluded from base policies.

## Twenty Minutes, Right Now

Pull up your policy document. Find the definitions section and the conditions section. Check three things:

1. Does your current security setup meet every condition listed?
2. Are the systems you actually use — especially cloud services — within the scope of the policy's definition of "system"?
3. Is there a breach response hotline number in the policy? Do you know where it is?
